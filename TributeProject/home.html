Dr. Norman Borlaug


A man with humble beginnings saving the lives of billions

Time Line
[Modified slightly from source: 3]

  • 1914 - Born in Cresco, Iowa
  • 1933 - Leaves his family's farm to attend the University of Minnesota.
  • 1935 - Has to stop school and save up more money.
  • 1937 - Finishes university and takes a job in the US Forestry Service
  • 1938 - Marries wife of 69 years Margret Gibson. Gets laid off due to budget cuts. Returns to school to study under Stakman, who teaches him about breeding pest-resistent plants.
  • 1941 - Tries to enroll in the military after the Pearl Harbor attack, but is rejected.
  • 1942 - Receives a Ph.D. in Genetics and Plant Pathology
  • 1944 - Rejects a 100% salary increase from Dupont, leaves behind his pregnant wife, and flies to Mexico to head a new plant pathology program. Over the next 16 years, his team breeds 6,000 different strains of disease resistent wheat - including different varieties for each major climate on Earth.
  • 1945 - Discovers a way to grown wheat twice each season, doubling wheat yields.
  • 1953 - crosses a short, sturdy dwarf breed of wheat with a high-yeidling American breed, creating a strain that responds well to fertilizer. It goes on to provide 95% of Mexico's wheat.
  • 1962 - Visits Delhi and brings his high-yielding strains of wheat to the Indian subcontinent in time to help mitigate mass starvation due to a rapidly expanding population
  • 1970 - receives the Nobel Peace Prize
  • 1984 - becomes a distinguished professor at Texas A&M University
  • 2009 - dies at the age of 95.

Personal Opinion

I learned about Dr. Norman Borlaugh after reading his book [2] as recommended in a blog post on Gates Notes talking about the history of crop growth in developing nations. What I found truly amazing is the similarity of his early career and cross-roads faced to what the average person experiences. He did not come from a priveleged position. When confronted with diverging paths that we see in our careers, he chose the humanitarian route. His decision to forego a significant salary increase during his wife's pregnancy to travel abroad in an effort to improve agriculture worldwide deserves an award on its own. To me that is a challenging decision and truly underscores his passion for making the world a better place. Even the story behind him learning about his nobel prize award is humbling (talked about in his biography [2]). He was working in the fields.
